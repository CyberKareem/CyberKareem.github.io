b:T4bc1,<p>I used to live inside my head, tormented by worries that never came true. Late at night, I would imagine worst-case scenarios about my job, relationships, everything, and inevitably work myself into a panic. One evening, while doom-scrolling on my phone, I stumbled on a simple Stoic quote by the ancient philosopher Seneca: “We suffer more often in imagination than in reality.”</p><p>That single line hit me like a revelation. It felt as if someone had articulated exactly what I was doing to myself. In that moment, I realized how my anxious mind was crafting phantom troubles, making me suffer needlessly. This was my introduction to Stoicism, and it marked the beginning of a gradual but profound shift in my mindset and lifestyle.</p><h2 id="discovering-ancient-wisdom-in-modern-times">Discovering Ancient Wisdom in Modern&nbsp;Times</h2><p>At first, I was skeptical that 2,000-year-old wisdom could help me with everyday stresses. But as I read more from Stoic thinkers like Seneca and Marcus Aurelius, their ideas resonated deeply. I started keeping a notebook of quotes that spoke to me.</p><h2 id="creating-your-own-luck">Creating Your Own&nbsp;Luck</h2><p>One of the first quotes I jotted down was Seneca’s reminder that “Luck is what happens when preparation meets opportunity.” It dawned on me that many things I labeled as “bad luck” in my life were at least partly within my control.</p><p>For example, I once failed an important exam and blamed it on bad luck, the questions were on topics I thought wouldn’t be covered, the timing was terrible, I’d had a bad night’s sleep. In hindsight, I hadn’t prepared thoroughly. I’d skipped chapters I deemed “unlikely” and crammed the night before instead of studying consistently. Seneca’s words encouraged me to take ownership of my outcomes. I began to see that by working hard and being ready, I could create my own “luck” when opportunities arose.</p><h2 id="from-imagination-to-reality">From Imagination to&nbsp;Reality</h2><p>When I first read Marcus Aurelius’s Meditations, I never imagined how much his Stoic wisdom would permeate my daily life. Marcus, a Roman emperor and philosopher, wrote personal notes to himself that now serve as timeless advice on how to live well. His teachings started guiding how I approached challenges each day, big and small.</p><figure><img src="/writing/media/4838bc48b25e93d1dea7c4d3.webp" alt="" width="1200" height="1600" loading="lazy" decoding="async"><figcaption><em>Standing in the Roman Forum, where Marcus Aurelius once walked and contemplated these very ideas that now guide my daily&nbsp;life</em></figcaption></figure><p>Importantly, Stoicism taught me a kind of emotional resilience I never had before. Seneca’s insight about imaginary suffering became a mantra whenever anxiety struck. Instead of spiraling into “what ifs,” I would literally say to myself: <em>We suffer more in imagination than in reality. Stay in the real.</em></p><p>It helped me catch myself when I started catastrophizing. With practice, I got better at distinguishing between actual problems and the awful stories my imagination was inventing. Most of the time, reality turned out far more manageable than the disasters I’d dreamed up.</p><h2 id="the-happiness-of-your-thoughts">The Happiness of Your&nbsp;Thoughts</h2><p>Marcus Aurelius's line, “The happiness of your life depends upon the quality of your thoughts,” made me examine how my inner dialogue affected my mood.</p><h2 id="why-the-voice-in-your-head-needs-a-rewrite">Why the Voice in Your Head Needs a&nbsp;Rewrite</h2><p>For instance, if I missed a deadline, my old pattern was to internally scream, “I’m such a failure, this is the worst!” That kind of thought would send me into a tailspin of shame. After absorbing Marcus’s wisdom, I worked on improving the quality of those thoughts. I learned to rephrase the narrative: “I missed a deadline; it’s not great, but I can work harder and fix this.” By framing setbacks as challenges instead of catastrophes, I remained calmer and more solution-focused.</p><p>I slowly realized something profound about how I valued myself. As I journaled about my day and my thoughts (inspired by the fact that Marcus Aurelius’s Meditations was basically his private journal), a line of reflection emerged in my mind: “We constantly measure our worth, but we are often not aware of how we do it.”</p><p>In other words, I was always judging myself, by career progress, by others’ opinions, by arbitrary benchmarks, without consciously deciding to do so. This unconscious yardstick was often negative and unrealistic. By becoming aware of it, I started redefining my metrics for self-worth.</p><h2 id="the-practice-of-daily-journaling">The Practice of Daily Journaling</h2><p>To actively cultivate better thoughts, I embraced daily journaling. Each night, I capture whatever is cluttering my mind, worries, hopes, things I’m grateful for. Sometimes I type into my Notion app, other times I scribble in a physical notebook, and on particularly exhausting days, I just voice-record my thoughts using Otter. This brain dump is cathartic; it clears out the gunk and often reveals patterns in my thinking.</p><p>If my thoughts are the seed of my happiness, journaling is the tool that helps me tend that inner garden. And indeed, on days when my mind is racing with worry, I’ve found that writing things out or sitting quietly to meditate can turn down the volume on the chaos.</p><h2 id="everything-is-opinion-not-fact">Everything Is Opinion, Not&nbsp;Fact</h2><p>One of the most liberating Stoic lessons I learned was not to take everything personally. Marcus Aurelius wisely noted: “Everything we hear is an opinion, not a fact. Everything we see is a perspective, not the truth.”</p><h2 id="the-power-of-mental-pause">The Power of Mental&nbsp;Pause</h2><p>Consider how often we get upset because of what someone said about us or how we perceived an event. I used to agonize over casual remarks, a friend’s teasing or a colleague’s critique, as if they were indisputable verdicts on my value. Stoicism taught me to insert a mental pause and remind myself: <em>That’s just their perspective or mood talking.</em></p><p>This mindset shift was life-changing in conflict situations. For example, after months of job hunting, I’d accumulated a string of rejections. My old reaction would’ve been to accept these as objective truths about my worth, spiraling into despair with each new rejection email. Instead, I recalled Marcus’s advice. I told myself, “These are opinions from brief interactions. Different companies, different interviewers, different perspectives, none of them the complete truth about who I am or what I can offer.” This helped me stay resilient and keep applying, rather than letting the rejections define me.</p><h2 id="turning-inward">Turning Inward</h2><p>This lesson also applies inwardly, to my own thoughts about myself. I used to have a very harsh inner critic that would state opinions like “You’re not good enough” as if they were facts. With Stoicism’s perspective, I learned to respond to that voice: “This is just a thought, not an objective fact.”</p><p>By treating my negative self-judgments as passing opinions, I could question and counter them. I cannot overstate how much lighter this made me feel. It’s like I finally gave myself permission not to believe every cruel thing I thought about myself.</p><h2 id="before-you-find-fault-look-in-the-mirror">Before You Find Fault, Look in the&nbsp;Mirror</h2><p>Another Marcus Aurelius gem that I carry with me is this humbling question: “Whenever you are about to find fault with someone, ask yourself the following question: What fault of mine most nearly resembles the one I am about to criticize?”</p><p>Ouch. That one stings in the best way possible.</p><h2 id="the-practice-of-empathy">The Practice of&nbsp;Empathy</h2><p>I remember getting irritated at a friend who cancelled plans last-minute. I was about to shoot off a passive-aggressive text/call when this Stoic question popped into my head. Had I ever, in my life, flaked on someone? The honest answer was yes, of course I had. We all have our off days.</p><p>Recognizing my own fault (I, too, had been unreliable at times) immediately softened my stance. Instead of blaming, I asked if they were okay and if we could reschedule. This not only preserved the friendship but also made me feel better than if I’d indulged in anger.</p><p>By routinely applying this principle, I noticed something amazing: my relationships improved. People can sense when you judge them, even if you don’t say it out loud. When I stopped mentally criticizing everyone for their faults, when I started extending the same compassion to others that I’d want for my shortcomings, conflicts diminished.</p><h2 id="wisdom-beyond-the-stoics-the-company-you-keep">Wisdom Beyond the Stoics: The Company You&nbsp;Keep</h2><p>Stoic writers formed the core of this practice, but I also found useful observations elsewhere. Niccolò Machiavelli wrote, “The first method for estimating the intelligence of a ruler is to look at the men he has around him.”</p><p>Now, I’m not a ruler (and have no ambitions to be one!), but this struck a chord on a personal level. It made me ask: Who do I surround myself with, and how are they influencing the person I am?</p><h2 id="the-people-around-you-shape-the-person-within">The People Around You Shape the Person&nbsp;Within</h2><p>In my younger years, I often spent time with friends who, frankly, fueled negative habits. Some were chronic complainers who saw the worst in everything; being around that mindset made me more pessimistic and bitter. Others had no ambitions, and in staying close to them I found it easy to stagnate and avoid challenges.</p><p>This realization was hard, because it meant distancing myself a bit from certain social circles. I didn’t “fire” my friends, but I did consciously seek out and spend more time with people who embodied qualities I admire. I joined Discord communities focused on career growth where members share the same aspirations, we celebrate each other’s certifications, share interview experiences and walkthrough challenges together. The accountability and mutual encouragement in these spaces was infectious. At work, I gravitated toward colleagues who were solution-oriented and calm under pressure.</p><p>Over time, I noticed my own habits changing. Just as negativity was contagious, so was optimism and drive. Little by little, I cultivated a supportive “inner circle” aligned with the values and mindset I aspired to. In turn, this made it much easier to live out the Stoic principles I was learning, it’s hard to stay level-headed and virtuous if you’re drowning in toxic company.</p><h2 id="building-daily-practices">Building Daily Practices</h2><p>Learning philosophical principles was one thing; applying them daily was another. Early on, I realized that if I wanted Stoicism’s benefits to stick, I’d have to reinforce them with consistent habits and practices.</p><figure><img src="/writing/media/1e776d69f97eafab0a34e379.webp" alt="" width="1200" height="1600" loading="lazy" decoding="async"><figcaption>Standing before David in Florence, I was struck by his expression, determined yet uncertain, the moment before facing Goliath. Michelangelo spent three years on this sculpture, showing up daily to chip away at marble. It reminded me that transformation isn’t about feeling ready or inspired; it’s about returning each day to the work, whether that’s journaling, meditating, or simply choosing better thoughts. We’re all just chiseling away at our own blocks of marble, one daily practice at a&nbsp;time.</figcaption></figure><h2 id="morning-and-evening-rituals">Morning and Evening&nbsp;Rituals</h2><p>One habit I developed is morning and evening reflection. This is deeply rooted in Stoic tradition. (Seneca, for example, would review his day each evening, examining what he did well and what he could improve.) In my case, each morning I take a few minutes to set intentions, I might re-read a favorite quote or remind myself, “Today, focus only on what you can control.” Each night, as I mentioned, I journal about how the day went.</p><h2 id="the-mind-body-connection">The Mind-Body Connection</h2><p>Another practice I swear by now is physical exercise, particularly when I’m in a mental funk. I even coined a personal mantra to summarize what I’ve learned: “When you feel off, turn your body on with exercise. When you overthink, your mind is on, turn it off with journaling or meditating.”</p><figure><img src="/writing/media/0bc685d866b19b64549368e2.webp" alt="" width="1200" height="1600" loading="lazy" decoding="async"><figcaption><em>Hiking in the Swiss Alps, where physical challenge meets mental&nbsp;clarity</em></figcaption></figure><p>What I mean is: if I’m feeling emotionally down, sluggish, unmotivated, often the best cure is to get my body moving. Hitting weights or even a brisk walk seems to flush out the mental cobwebs. On the flip side, if my mind is overactive, racing with thoughts at 2 AM, I know I need to quiet it with introspective activity.</p><p>This mind-body approach keeps me balanced. Interestingly, Stoicism advocates caring for both body and mind, treating both as instruments to be kept in good condition. Marcus Aurelius himself exercised and trained physically, but he also cautioned not to let the body’s urges rule the mind.</p><h2 id="meditation-and-mindfulness">Meditation and Mindfulness</h2><p>Incorporating moments of quiet meditation or mindful breathing each day became one of the most tangible ways I applied Stoic principles. While ancient Stoics didn’t practice meditation in the Buddhist sense, they did emphasize mindful presence and rational observation of one’s own mind.</p><p>Even a brief meditation session, sitting calmly and watching my breath, helps me live out Stoic ideas. It trains me to notice when a strong emotion or thought is rising and then respond deliberately rather than react impulsively. That small gap of awareness is often enough to prevent me from losing my cool.</p><h2 id="a-cosmic-perspective-on-life">A Cosmic Perspective on&nbsp;Life</h2><figure><img src="/writing/media/1b1a11929005846793856a3a.webp" alt="" width="1200" height="1600" loading="lazy" decoding="async"><figcaption><em>The Matterhorn at sunset, a reminder of our beautiful insignificance in the grand scheme of the&nbsp;cosmos</em></figcaption></figure><p>One of the most awe-inspiring shifts in my mindset came when I zoomed out to the big picture. It happened when I encountered a quote from physicist Lawrence M. Krauss: “Every atom in your body came from a star that exploded. And, the atoms in your left hand probably came from a different star than your right hand. It really is the most poetic thing I know about physics: You are all stardust. You couldn’t be here if stars hadn’t exploded, because the elements, the carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, iron, all the things that matter for evolution and for life, weren’t created at the beginning of time. They were created in the nuclear furnaces of stars, and the only way for them to get into your body is if those stars were kind enough to explode…The stars died so that you could be here today.”</p><p>I remember the chills I felt when I first heard that. The imagery of stars exploding eons ago, forging the elements that now make up my body, it was stunning. Suddenly, my perspective on life’s problems expanded. In the grand scheme of the cosmos, many of my daily anxieties looked… well, tiny.</p><h2 id="stardust-and-stoicism">Stardust and&nbsp;Stoicism</h2><p>This cosmic perspective echoed something I’d felt in Marcus Aurelius’s writings too. He often reflects on how small we are in the flow of time and the universe. (Marcus wrote things like, “Observe the stars in their course, and imagine yourself running alongside them.”) He knew that contemplating nature can shrink our egos and trivial concerns.</p><p>Realizing that I am literally stardust, a composite of ancient star remnants, was strangely uplifting. It made me feel connected to something vast and magnificent. It also made trivial inconveniences, a rude message/email, a traffic jam, seem hardly worth losing serenity over.</p><p>On days when I feel overwhelmed by life, I’ll step outside at night for a quick “reality check” with the stars. I’ll remind myself that the carbon in my muscles, the calcium in my bones, the iron in my blood, all were born in stars long before Earth existed. It doesn’t erase my problems, but it shrinks them to scale.</p><h2 id="what-changed">What changed</h2><p>Writing this now, I can truly appreciate how much my mindset and lifestyle have evolved. The philosophy of Stoicism, bolstered by insights from other thinkers, has gently woven itself into the fabric of my everyday life. It didn’t happen overnight; there was no single thunderbolt moment where I became a perfect Stoic (and I’m still not one).</p><p>Instead, it was a slow accumulation of small lessons:</p><ul><li>I learned to prepare and do my best, then not fret about outcomes beyond my control</li><li>I learned that tending to the quality of my thoughts directly improves the quality of my days</li><li>I learned not to believe every negative thought or take every comment to heart</li><li>I learned to pause before judging others, remembering my own flaws</li><li>I learned to surround myself with good influences and nurture grounding habits</li><li>I learned to zoom out when crushed by problems, remembering our star-born context</li></ul><p>The result? I feel more centered and resilient than I used to. Challenges that would have floored the “old me” still arrive, but now I face them with tools in hand and centuries-old sage advice in my ear.</p><h2 id="a-practical-starting-point">A practical starting point</h2><p>If you’re curious about exploring Stoicism yourself, here’s a simple way to begin:</p><h2 id="start-with-one-practice">Start with one practice:</h2><ul><li>Morning intention: Take 2 minutes each morning to remind yourself: “Today I’ll focus on what I can control”</li><li>Evening reflection: Before bed, ask yourself: “What went well today? What could I improve?”</li><li>The pause: When upset, take a breath and ask: “Is this fact or opinion?”</li></ul><h2 id="read-one-book">Read one book:</h2><ul><li>For beginners: “A Guide to the Good Life” by William Irvine</li><li>For the source: “Meditations” by Marcus Aurelius (Gregory Hays translation)</li><li>For practical wisdom: “Letters from a Stoic” by Seneca</li></ul><h2 id="remember-one-quote-pick-one-that-resonates-and-make-it-your-companion-for-a-week-mine-was-seneca-s-we-suffer-more-in-imagination-than-in-reality">Remember one quote: Pick one that resonates and make it your companion for a week. Mine was Seneca’s “We suffer more in imagination than in reality.”</h2><p>Life will no doubt continue to throw curveballs, but thanks to Seneca, Marcus, and even a stellar explosion or two, I feel ready to meet them with a calmer mind, an open heart, and a spirit that refuses to quit.</p>6:["$","div",null,{"className":"article-prose","dangerouslySetInnerHTML":{"__html":"$b"}}]
