INE recently bought up Pentester Academy too.</p><h2 id="course"><strong>Course</strong></h2><p>The Penetration Testing Professional (PTP) course is a beginner course for Penetration Testers and IT Security Professionals and ends with an exam and a certification (eCPPTv2). eLearnSecurity / INE designed the course for people with knowledge in IT, but on a basic level. If someone is not familiar with IT and Linux, then you can pick the Penetration Tester Student (PTS) first, to build a solid foundation.</p><p>The PTP course offers 6500+ slides, 17+ hours of video training material and 27 labs.</p><h2 id="exam"><strong>Exam</strong></h2><p>The exam could be started on demand. After starting, I received the scope, rules of engagement, reporting requirements, and VPN configuration.</p><figure><img src="/writing/media/4404dab9b05a9a2be4e61ff9.webp" alt="eCPPTv2 Certification Review, figure 3" width="461" height="54" loading="lazy" decoding="async"></figure><p>Then you have one week to compromise the targets in scope, as well as another week to complete a professional report and upload it for an examiner review.</p><p>The scope is straightforward. The test is almost split into two parts; external and internal. The initial external infrastructure is a lot of fun. Once you’re inside the network, it’s up to you to compromise the visible infrastructure in scope, escalate privileges and report on any vulnerability you find.</p><p>Overall, the exam environment was very stable, and I experienced no issues with connectivity or similar.</p><h2 id="content"><strong>Content</strong></h2><p>If you’re new to penetration testing, you’ll probably struggle with this exam. There is one task in particular that had me scratching my head for a while. I used a whiteboard for mind-mapping purposes throughout the exam.</p><p>During the external penetration test, you’ll encounter multiple vulnerabilities. In the end, I found three different ways to get a shell on the underlying system.</p><p>You’ll have to pivot to other domains and subnets and exploit the vulnerabilities present.</p><h2 id="reporting">Reporting</h2><p>My report was 81 pages long, with a lot of screenshots, executive summary, report structure, contents page, etc. eLearnSecurity is clear on the reporting requirements. I made sure I explained every vulnerability I found, with screenshots and remediation steps.</p><figure><img src="/writing/media/73a0eaec03f825efd37bd4f9.webp" alt="eCPPTv2 Certification Review, figure 4" width="879" height="528" loading="lazy" decoding="async"></figure><h2 id="recommendations">Recommendations</h2><ul><li>Take your time. Don’t underestimate the requirements of the exam, it’s not a CTF. You have seven days to compromise, plus another seven to report.</li><li>Enumerate your targets carefully. Again, take your time here and Google if you are not sure.</li><li>Report every vulnerability you encounter. Like a real-life engagement, you’ll want to inform your client of their technical debt and security posture. Even if it doesn’t lead to a root shell or even code execution, you’ll want to include it in your report.</li><li>Take a lot of screenshots.</li><li>I used Notion as a note-taking tool throughout the exam (It would be better for me to use OneNote as it was easier and time effective for the reporting phase).</li><li>Don’t underestimate the post-exploitation phase.</li><li>Ensure you know how to pivot. check this out: <a href="https://pentest.blog/explore-hidden-networks-with-double-pivoting/" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Explore Hidden Networks With Double Pivoting - Pentest Blog</a>.</li><li>Alongside your Kali VM, ensure you have a Windows VM to hand, with Immunity Debugger (with Mona script) installed and ready to go.</li><li>Very detailed step-by-step
